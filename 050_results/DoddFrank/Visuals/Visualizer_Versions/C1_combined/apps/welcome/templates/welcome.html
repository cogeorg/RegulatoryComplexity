In 2012, Andrew Haldane declared during his The Dog and the Frisbee speach at the Economic Policy Symposion - Jackson Hole that the type of complex regulation introduced over recent decades may not only be costly and cumbersome but also sub-optimal for crisis control. So far, there has been one attempt to measure this complexity in legal texts. In their paper Law is Code: A Software Engineering Approach to Analyzing the United States Code, Li et al. measured complexity by the number of words and word changes, the number of cross-references, and the number of conditional statements and found an enormous increase in complexity over the last century.

This project aims to measure the regulatory complexity of the Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (short Dodd–Frank Act). Therefore, we follow two approaches:

The Halstead Approach

This approach consideres text as being an algorithm containing operators and operands on the basis of which one can compute complexity measures like Volume, Difficulty and Effort. In order to do so, individual words or terms of the Dodd-Frank Act need to be classified according to their grammatical and regulatory purpose.

Regulatory Coherence

This approach aims at measurung the coherence of the Dodd-Frank Act. Based on dependency parses and / or word vectors, we will create a network of the sentences of the Dodd-Frank Act. Therefore, we have to distinguish between regulating entities, regulated entities, definitions and references.
